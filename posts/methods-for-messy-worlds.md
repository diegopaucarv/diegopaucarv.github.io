# Methods for Messy Worlds

The current enthusiasm for large language models in social science worries me — not because the tools are not powerful, but because the enthusiasm tends to outrun the epistemology.

## The Seduction of Scale

There is something intoxicating about the idea that if you have enough data, the patterns will speak for themselves. This is, to be direct, not how knowledge works. Data does not speak. Data is mute. We speak, through our choices about what to measure, how to aggregate, and which comparisons to draw.

Scale amplifies signal. It also amplifies noise, and it amplifies the researcher's assumptions at every stage of the pipeline.

## What Mixed Methods Gives You

I am a methodological pluralist. I use quantitative tools when I want to measure the prevalence or distribution of something. I use qualitative tools when I want to understand the mechanisms — why something happens, not just whether it does.

The two are not in competition. They operate at different levels of abstraction. The error is to use one when you need the other.

## A Heuristic

Before choosing a method, I ask: what is the nature of my ignorance?

- If I don't know **how much**, I need a number.
- If I don't know **why**, I need a conversation.
- If I don't know **whether my categories are even the right ones**, I need to sit with the data before I impose any structure at all.

This is not a new insight. It is an old one that keeps needing to be restated.
